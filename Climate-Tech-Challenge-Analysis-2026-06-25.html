Executive answer

Your data-analysis idea can be effective enough for the Oxford Saïd Global Climate Tech Challenge, but only if you avoid a generic “climate dashboard.” The strongest approach is to turn weather, satellite, exposure, vulnerability and infrastructure data into actionable risk decisions: who is exposed, where, when, and what intervention reduces harm fastest.

1. Background: why this topic matters

The scientific basis is strong. The IPCC AR6 states that human-induced climate change is already affecting weather and climate extremes in every region, with stronger evidence since AR5 for changes in heatwaves, heavy precipitation, droughts and tropical cyclones. [IPCC] The IPCC’s extreme-events chapter assesses temperature extremes, heavy precipitation, river floods, droughts, storms, tropical cyclones, and compound events, which means your theme is not a marginal topic. It sits directly inside mainstream climate-risk science. [IPCC]

The practical need is also clear. WMO’s State of the Global Climate 2024 reports that 2024 was the warmest year in the 175-year observational record and that extreme weather caused major economic and social disruption. [World Meteorological Organization] Heat is especially important: WHO reports that studies for 2000-2019 estimate about 489,000 heat-related deaths per year, with 45% in Asia and 36% in Europe. [World Health Organization]

The “prevention” side is where your project can score. UNDRR says early warning and anticipatory action are among the most proven and cost-effective disaster-risk-reduction measures. [UNDRR] WMO cites analysis from the Global Commission on Adaptation suggesting that every US\(1 invested in early-warning systems can produce about US\)9 in net economic benefits. [World Meteorological Organization] A World Bank paper estimated benefit-cost ratios for hydrometeorological early-warning investments between 4 and 36. [Open Knowledge Repository]

The gap remains large. In 2024, 108 countries reported some multi-hazard early-warning-system capacity, which was 55% of countries and more than double the number in 2015. [UNDRR] By 2025, WMO/UNDRR reported 119 countries, or 60% of all countries, with a multi-hazard early warning system, but coverage gaps remained, especially in small island developing states. [World Meteorological Organization]

2. Core scientific mechanism

Extreme-weather risk is not just “bad weather.” In disaster-risk science, the useful model is:

Risk = Hazard × Exposure × Vulnerability ÷ Coping capacity

For your project, that means:

ComponentMeaningExample
HazardThe physical eventHeatwave, flood, drought, storm, wildfire
ExposurePeople/assets in the hazard zonePopulation, schools, hospitals, roads, farms
VulnerabilityWhy some people/assets suffer moreAge, poverty, housing quality, disability, lack of cooling, weak drainage
Coping capacityAbility to prepare/respond/recoverShelters, emergency services, warnings, evacuation routes, insurance, water storage

This is why simple prediction is weaker than impact-based prediction. A 42°C day in a wealthy, shaded, air-conditioned neighborhood is dangerous. The same 42°C day in an informal settlement with metal roofs, elderly residents, no trees and limited water can be deadly.

Your project should therefore predict impact, not only weather.

3. How effective will your data analysis be?

My assessment: effective, if you choose the right task

Data analysis is very effective for:

  1. Identifying hotspots
    • Which neighborhoods repeatedly flood?
    • Which schools experience the worst heat exposure?
    • Which roads become unusable after heavy rainfall?
    • Which farms show early drought stress?
  1. Combining hazard and vulnerability
    • Heat + elderly population + poor housing
    • Flood + low elevation + poor drainage + dense population
    • Wildfire + dry vegetation + wind + road access difficulty
  1. Early warning and prioritization
    • Which communities should receive alerts first?
    • Where should emergency water, cooling centers or pumps be placed?
    • Which road repairs restore access to the most people?
  1. Evaluating adaptation measures
    • Where would cool roofs reduce heat exposure most?
    • Where would permeable pavement reduce runoff most?
    • Where should low-cost flood sensors be installed?
    • Which firebreaks or vegetation-management zones protect the most buildings?

Data analysis is less effective for:

  1. Inventing better global weather forecasts from scratch
    • National meteorological agencies and ECMWF-style systems already use physics-based numerical weather prediction and massive data assimilation.
    • A student challenge project is unlikely to beat them at raw storm-track prediction.
  1. Predicting exact disaster losses without local data
    • Damage depends on building quality, insurance, emergency behavior, local drainage and governance.
    • Many places have weak loss records.
  1. Rare-event machine learning
    • Extreme events are rare, imbalanced and non-stationary.
    • Climate change shifts the probability distribution, so past events are not a perfect training set.

Best strategy: do not claim “we predict disasters perfectly.” Claim: “We transform public climate, satellite and community data into earlier, fairer, more actionable preparedness decisions.”

That is credible.

4. Strong project directions for the challenge

Option A: Heatwave Resilience Tool for schools, clinics and vulnerable neighborhoods

This is probably the highest-feasibility direction.

What it does

A platform that identifies urban heat-risk hotspots and recommends adaptation actions:

  • cool roofs
  • shade trees
  • reflective pavements
  • water points
  • cooling centers
  • school schedule changes
  • SMS warnings for high-risk residents

Why it is strong

Heat data is accessible, health impact is serious, and interventions are understandable. WHO’s heat-health data gives you a strong public-health justification. [World Health Organization] ERA5-HEAT provides historical thermal comfort indices derived from ERA5, including indicators for outdoor human thermal stress. [Climate Data Store] Landsat 8 Level 2 provides land surface temperature and surface reflectance, useful for mapping urban heat islands. [Google for Developers]

Strong finding you can test

“Neighborhoods with low vegetation, high impervious surface, dense population and high social vulnerability experience disproportionate heat risk, and targeted cool-roof deployment can reduce exposure more efficiently than random deployment.”

Cool roofs are a credible material intervention. The U.S. EPA states that cool roofs reduce heat transfer into buildings and can reduce peak cooling demand in air-conditioned residential buildings by 11-27%. [US EPA] Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory explains that high solar reflectance and high thermal emittance are the core physical properties that make a roof stay cooler. [Heat Island Group]

[SPEI] The SPEIbase global drought database provides monthly global drought conditions at 0.5° resolution from 1901 onward. [SPEI] GRACE and GRACE-FO data can track terrestrial water storage anomalies from 2002 to present, with applications in assessing drought and flood magnitude and frequency. [Climate Data Store]

Option E: Post-disaster recovery prioritization tool

This is a less common but strong idea.

What it does

After a flood, storm or wildfire, it ranks where to send recovery resources first:

  • blocked roads
  • damaged bridges
  • isolated villages
  • damaged clinics
  • damaged schools
  • electricity or water-system disruption

Why it is strong

Many projects focus on prediction. Fewer focus on recovery speed, which the challenge explicitly mentions. You can use satellite imagery, OpenStreetMap roads, WorldPop population and critical-facility data to estimate which repairs restore access to the most people.

[NASA Earthdata] The Humanitarian Data Exchange provides crisis and humanitarian datasets for response and decision-making. [Humanitarian Data Exchange] Humanitarian OpenStreetMap states that open map data supports disaster management, risk reduction and SDG-related work. [hotosm.org]

15. Recommended final project framing

Use this structure in your challenge submission:

Problem

Extreme weather warnings often describe the hazard but not the local impact. Communities need to know who is exposed, which assets are vulnerable, and which action should be taken first.

Solution

A data-driven resilience platform that combines climate forecasts, satellite observations, population exposure, social vulnerability and infrastructure data to produce actionable preparedness and recovery recommendations.

Innovation

The system moves from weather forecasting to impact-based decision support. It also links data analytics with sensors and material interventions such as cool roofs, permeable pavements or fire-resistant surfaces.

Prototype

Start with one hazard:

  • HeatShield for heatwaves, or

Khujand is in Sughd Region in northern Tajikistan. It has hot, dry summers and is located in a valley/lowland setting with likely strong urban heat contrasts between built surfaces, bare soil, irrigated/vegetated areas and the Syr Darya river corridor.

- There is a documented lack of local data connecting heatwaves to morbidity and mortality.
- Urban heat islands can be missed by official weather stations because stations often do not represent dense urban streets.
- Khujand has useful urban contrasts: dense neighborhoods, markets, roads, public institutions, bare land, river corridor and vegetation.

Strong framing:
> Tajikistan has heat warnings. What it lacks is high-resolution local action intelligence.

## 5. Core scientific mechanism for heat risk

Heat risk is not only air temperature. It depends on:
- air temperature
- humidity
- wind
- solar radiation
- nighttime cooling
- land surface temperature
- built-up density
- vegetation/shade
- housing material
- population age structure
- health vulnerability
- access to cooling and water
- clinic capacity
- ability to receive warnings

Urban heat island mechanism:
- Concrete, asphalt and roofs absorb solar radiation.
- Dense urban surfaces store heat during the day.
- At night, stored heat is released slowly.
- Low tree cover reduces shade and evapotranspiration.
- Weak ventilation corridors reduce cooling.
- Poorly insulated buildings increase indoor heat exposure.

Human health mechanism:
- The body cools through sweating and blood-flow regulation.
- High humidity reduces sweat evaporation.
- Hot nights reduce physiological recovery.
- Children, elderly people, pregnant women, outdoor workers and people with chronic diseases are more vulnerable.
- Heat can worsen cardiovascular, renal and respiratory stress.
